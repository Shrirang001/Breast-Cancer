Section 01

Pathology & Disease Definition

Foundational biology an RWE analyst needs to contextualize data and study design decisions.

What is Breast Cancer?

Breast cancer is a malignant neoplasm originating from epithelial cells of the breast — primarily the ductal and lobular cells of the mammary gland. It arises when normal regulatory mechanisms controlling cell proliferation, differentiation, and apoptosis are disrupted through accumulation of somatic mutations and epigenetic alterations.

  • Ductal carcinoma — originates in the milk ducts; most common (~80% of cases)
  • Lobular carcinoma — originates in milk-producing glands (lobules); ~10–15%
  • Other rare types — inflammatory, Paget's disease, phyllodes tumor

In Situ vs. Invasive

  • DCIS (Ductal Carcinoma In Situ) — confined to duct, has not breached basement membrane; Stage 0
  • LCIS (Lobular Carcinoma In Situ) — marker of risk, not true malignancy
  • Invasive/Infiltrating — cancer has broken through basement membrane into surrounding tissue; capable of metastasis

Metastatic Disease (mBC)

  • Cancer has spread beyond the breast and regional lymph nodes to distant organs
  • Most common metastatic sites: bone, liver, lung, brain
  • mBC is generally considered incurable — treatment goals shift to disease control, symptom management, and quality of life
  • mTNBC has the worst prognosis of all metastatic subtypes — median OS ~12–18 months in pre-immunotherapy era

Key Pathologic Biomarkers — Why They Matter for RWE

In RWE, biomarker data comes from pathology reports within EHR systems. Understanding these markers helps analysts define study cohorts correctly.

ER / PR

Estrogen/Progesterone Receptor. Assessed by IHC. ≥1% positive = HR+. Drives endocrine therapy decisions.

HER2

Human Epidermal Growth Factor Receptor 2. IHC 3+ or FISH amplified = HER2+. Targetable with HER2-directed agents.

Ki-67

Proliferation index. High Ki-67 (>20%) = aggressive tumor. Used to distinguish Luminal A vs B.

💡
RWE Analyst Note: In Flatiron data, biomarker status is extracted from unstructured pathology notes using NLP. Always account for missing biomarker data and potential misclassification bias when defining TNBC cohorts. Sensitivity analyses with varying definitions are common.
Section 02

Molecular Subtypes

The three clinically actionable subtypes — and why subtype determines everything from study eligibility to drug exposure definition.

HR+ / HER2− (Hormone Receptor Positive)

ER+ and/or PR+ · HER2− · ~70% of all breast cancers

Biology: Tumor growth driven by estrogen/progesterone signaling. Endocrine therapy exploits this dependency. Generally slower-growing, better prognosis.

  • Luminal A: Low Ki-67, ER+/PR+ high, better prognosis
  • Luminal B: Higher Ki-67, may be HER2+, more aggressive

RWE Relevance: CDK4/6 inhibitor real-world effectiveness studies. Large patient population — ideal for treatment patterns research. Defining 1L vs 2L requires careful index date logic around ET start.

HER2+ (HER2 Overexpressed)

HER2 IHC 3+ or FISH amplified · ~15–20% of breast cancers

Biology: Amplification of ERBB2 gene drives aggressive tumor growth. Highly targetable with trastuzumab, pertuzumab, T-DM1, T-DXd. HER2-low (IHC 1+ or 2+/FISH−) is an emerging subtype.

RWE Relevance: Real-world uptake of T-DXd after approval (2022). Defining HER2+ in claims vs EHR. HER2-low identification requires careful NLP-extracted IHC score data from Flatiron.

TNBC (Triple-Negative Breast Cancer)

ER− · PR− · HER2− · ~15% of breast cancers · Focus of this guide

Biology: Lacks all three targetable receptors. Historically treated with chemotherapy only. Highly heterogeneous — includes basal-like, immunomodulatory, mesenchymal subtypes (Lehmann classification).

  • Disproportionately affects younger, premenopausal women
  • Higher rates in Black/African American women
  • BRCA1/2 mutations more prevalent (~10–20%)
  • PD-L1 expression: ~40% of mTNBC (key for IO eligibility)

mTNBC Clinical Context:

  • Median OS: ~12–18 months (chemo era); improving with IO/ADCs
  • High rates of visceral metastases (liver, lung)
  • CNS metastases in ~30% of patients
  • Rapid progression common — short time to next treatment
⚠
RWE Cohort Definition: TNBC is defined by absence of markers. In Flatiron, this requires confirmed negative status for all three — not just missing data. Distinguish "not tested" from "confirmed negative."

TNBC Subtyping (Lehmann Classification — RWE Context)

Molecular subtyping by gene expression. Rarely available in real-world data but useful to know for interview context.

BL1 / BL2

Basal-like 1&2. High cell cycle/DNA damage genes. Most responsive to platinum agents.

M / MSL

Mesenchymal. Stem cell-like features. May respond to PI3K/mTOR inhibitors.

IM

Immunomodulatory. Higher TILs, PD-L1 expression. Best candidates for immunotherapy.

AJCC 8th Edition Update (2018): Biomarker-modified anatomic staging was introduced — ER, PR, HER2, and grade now modify pathologic stage group. De Novo vs. Recurrent Metastatic — Key Distinction for RWE

De Novo Stage IV — patient presents with metastatic disease at initial diagnosis. No prior early-stage breast cancer treatment. Approximately 6–10% of all breast cancer diagnoses.

Recurrent Metastatic — patient previously treated for early-stage BC, later develops metastatic recurrence. May have had prior chemo, radiation, surgery. Affects line of therapy counting in RWE.

⚠
RWE Design Decision: In a treatment patterns study, you must decide whether to include both de novo and recurrent mTNBC, or one only. Prior treatment history (neoadjuvant/adjuvant chemo) impacts what counts as "1L metastatic." Always define your index date and prior therapy washout window explicitly.
Section 04

Treatment Modalities

All treatment approaches used in breast cancer, with focus on their relevance to mTNBC and RWE study design.

Surgery

  • Lumpectomy (BCS) — breast-conserving surgery; removes tumor + margin. Requires radiation follow-up.
  • Mastectomy — full breast removal. Simple, modified radical, or bilateral prophylactic.
  • Axillary lymph node dissection (ALND) — removes lymph nodes if positive
  • Sentinel lymph node biopsy (SLNB) — less invasive nodal assessment
📌
mTNBC context: Surgery in metastatic setting is rare/palliative. Relevant mainly for recurrent mBC (prior surgical history affects line counting).

Radiation Therapy

  • Whole breast RT — after lumpectomy; standard of care
  • Chest wall RT — after mastectomy in high-risk patients
  • Palliative RT — bone mets, brain mets in mBC setting
  • SBRT/SRS — stereotactic radiosurgery for CNS metastases
📌
RWE Note: Radiation may appear as a procedure code in Flatiron/claims. In mTNBC treatment patterns studies, palliative RT is usually not considered a "line of therapy" but should be captured as a concomitant treatment.

Chemotherapy — Backbone of mTNBC Treatment

1L

Taxane-Based ± Immunotherapy (PD-L1+ patients)

Nab-paclitaxel + atezolizumab (IMpassion130) or pembrolizumab + chemo (KEYNOTE-522 neoadj / KEYNOTE-355 metastatic). Carboplatin combinations used in BRCA-mutated patients.

Nab-paclitaxel Paclitaxel Carboplatin Atezolizumab Pembrolizumab
2L

Sacituzumab Govitecan (ADC) or Alternative Chemo

Sacituzumab govitecan (Trodelvy) — FDA approved 2021 for mTNBC after ≥2 prior therapies (ASCENT trial). Capecitabine, eribulin, gemcitabine also used.

Sacituzumab govitecan Capecitabine Eribulin Gemcitabine
3L+

Later Lines — Limited Options, Investigational Agents

Ixabepilone, vinorelbine, cisplatin. PARP inhibitors (olaparib, talazoparib) for gBRCA1/2-mutated patients (any line). Clinical trials are an important pathway.

Ixabepilone Vinorelbine Olaparib Talazoparib

Targeted Therapy in TNBC

  • PARP inhibitors (olaparib, talazoparib) — for gBRCA1/2 mutations; FDA approved for mTNBC
  • ADCs — Sacituzumab govitecan (TROP-2 targeted); datopotamab deruxtecan (emerging)
  • PI3K/AKT pathway — capivasertib (AKT inhibitor); relevant in PIK3CA/AKT/PTEN-altered tumors
  • Androgen receptor (AR) — LAR subtype; enzalutamide under investigation

Immunotherapy in mTNBC

  • Pembrolizumab (Keytruda) — PD-1 inhibitor. Approved 1L mTNBC for CPS≥10 (KEYNOTE-355)
  • Atezolizumab (Tecentriq) — PD-L1 inhibitor. Approval withdrawn in US (2021) after IMpassion130 issues in ITT population
  • PD-L1 testing — SP142 assay for atezolizumab; 22C3 assay for pembrolizumab; CPS vs IC scoring matters
  • RWE Note: IO eligibility (PD-L1+ status) must be captured from Flatiron structured/NLP fields

Line of Therapy (LOT) — RWE Definition Logic

Defining lines of therapy is one of the most critical and contested steps in any treatment patterns study. There is no universal standard — you must justify your approach.

1

Identify Metastatic Index Date

Date of first recorded Stage IV diagnosis or first evidence of metastatic disease in Flatiron (structured stage field or NLP-extracted from provider notes). For recurrent mBC: date of recurrence diagnosis.

2

Define 1L Start Date

First systemic therapy initiation on or after the metastatic index date. Allow a look-back window (typically 30–60 days before index) to capture treatment started in anticipation of confirmed stage IV.

3

Detect Line Breaks

A new line begins when: (a) a new drug class is added that wasn't part of current regimen, (b) previous regimen stopped and new drugs started after a gap (e.g., ≥30–90 days), or (c) a drug outside current regimen is added after progression evidence. Combination regimens (e.g., pembrolizumab + nab-paclitaxel) count as one line.

4

Handle Maintenance Therapy

Maintenance (e.g., pembrolizumab continued after chemo stops) is typically considered part of the same LOT, not a new line. Define this explicitly in your SAP.

Pseudocode — Line of Therapy Assignment SQL Logic 
-- Step 1: Get metastatic index date per patient
WITH met_index AS (
  SELECT
    patient_id,
    MIN(diagnosis_date) AS met_index_date
  FROM flatiron.cancer_diagnosis
  WHERE
    cancer_type    = 'Breast'
    AND stage_group  = 'IV'     -- or use metastatic_flag = 1
    AND er_status   = 'Negative'
    AND pr_status   = 'Negative'
    AND her2_status = 'Negative'
  GROUP BY patient_id
),

-- Step 2: Get all drug administrations after met_index_date
drug_admin AS (
  SELECT
    d.patient_id,
    d.drug_name,
    d.administration_date,
    m.met_index_date
  FROM flatiron.drug_administration d
  JOIN met_index m ON d.patient_id = m.patient_id
  WHERE d.administration_date >= DATEADD(day, -30, m.met_index_date)
    AND d.drug_category = 'Systemic Antineoplastic'
),

-- Step 3: Detect line breaks using gap logic
-- Flag new line when gap > 90 days and new drug class detected
line_breaks AS (
  SELECT
    patient_id,
    drug_name,
    administration_date,
    LAG(administration_date) OVER (
      PARTITION BY patient_id ORDER BY administration_date
    ) AS prev_admin_date,
    DATEDIFF(day,
      LAG(administration_date) OVER (
        PARTITION BY patient_id ORDER BY administration_date),
      administration_date
    ) AS gap_days
  FROM drug_admin
),

SELECT
  patient_id,
  drug_name,
  administration_date,
  CASE
    WHEN gap_days > 90 THEN 'NEW_LINE'
    WHEN gap_days IS NULL THEN 'LINE_1_START'
    ELSE 'CONTINUATION'
  END AS line_flag
FROM line_breaks;

  • Asian women: lower BC overall, but when diagnosed, higher HER2+ proportion

Section 07

Efficacy Parameters

Clinical trial endpoints and their real-world equivalents — how we measure effectiveness in mTNBC studies.

OS
Overall Survival

Time from a defined start point (e.g., treatment initiation or diagnosis) to death from any cause. The gold standard efficacy endpoint in oncology.

RWE Proxy: In Flatiron, death is captured from social security death master file (SSDMF) linkage and EHR records. However, completeness can be <100%. Use last known alive date as censoring point for patients not known to have died. Report median OS with Kaplan-Meier curves.
PFS
Progression-Free Survival

Time from treatment start to objective disease progression (per RECIST) or death. Requires imaging data — the most challenging to capture in RWE.

RWE Challenge: Radiographic progression is rarely structured in EHR. RWE proxies for PFS include: time to next treatment (TTNT), time to treatment discontinuation (TTD), or NLP-extracted progression from radiology notes. Each has different biases — document which you use.
ORR
Objective Response Rate

Proportion of patients achieving complete response (CR) or partial response (PR) per RECIST criteria. Typically assessed from imaging at protocol-defined timepoints in trials.

RWE Proxy: NLP-extracted response from radiology reports in Flatiron enhanced dataset. "Stable disease," "partial response," "progressive disease" — extractable but with variable accuracy. Often reported as descriptive rather than primary endpoint in RWE.
PR
Partial Response

≥30% decrease in sum of longest diameters of target lesions per RECIST 1.1. Patient has responded to treatment but not achieved complete eradication.

RWE Note: Partial response is a component of ORR (CR + PR). In mTNBC, ORR with sacituzumab govitecan was ~35% (ASCENT). Real-world ORR may differ due to patient heterogeneity and later-line use.
pCR
Pathologic Complete Response

No residual invasive cancer in the breast and axillary lymph nodes at the time of surgery after neoadjuvant therapy. Defined as ypT0/is ypN0. Surrogate endpoint for EFS/OS in early-stage TNBC.

RWE Setting: pCR is an early-stage/neoadjuvant endpoint. In mTNBC treatment patterns studies (metastatic setting), pCR is not applicable but may appear in patient history (prior neoadjuvant setting). Patients who did NOT achieve pCR after neoadjuvant chemo are now eligible for capecitabine (CREATE-X) or olaparib (OlympiAD context).
TTNT
Time to Next Treatment

Time from start of a given line of therapy to initiation of the next line. A surrogate for PFS in RWE when imaging-based progression is unavailable.

RWE Primary Endpoint: TTNT is commonly the primary effectiveness endpoint in RWE treatment patterns studies. Advantages: fully captured from drug administration records. Limitations: can't distinguish discontinuation due to toxicity vs. progression. Must acknowledge.

Section 08

RWE Project Walkthrough — mTNBC Treatment Patterns

Full study design, rationale, cohort build, exposure definition, and outcomes logic using Flatiron Health data.

Project Title & Business Question

"Real-World Treatment Patterns, Time to Next Treatment, and Overall Survival Among Patients with Metastatic Triple-Negative Breast Cancer Treated in US Community Oncology Settings"

Business/Scientific Question: How are patients with mTNBC actually being treated in the real world across lines of therapy? How long do they stay on each treatment? What is real-world OS? How does treatment use differ pre- vs. post-sacituzumab govitecan approval (April 2021)?

Why RWE? Why Not an RCT?

Limitations of RCTs in mTNBC

  • Strict eligibility criteria exclude elderly, comorbid, poor PS patients — typical in real world
  • Protocol-defined dosing ≠ real-world dose modifications
  • RCTs don't capture treatment sequencing across all lines
  • Time lag: ASCENT published 2021; what happened before and during approval?
  • RCTs can't answer: who receives which drugs in which order after approval?

What RWE Adds

  • Captures actual treatment patterns including off-label use
  • Reflects real-world patient population (older, sicker, diverse)
  • Enables pre/post approval analyses
  • Can assess time to next treatment, treatment duration, dose modifications
  • Informs payer coverage decisions, formulary placement, label expansions
  • Supports Health Technology Assessment (HTA) submissions

Study Design Overview

Design: Retrospective cohort study

Data Source: Flatiron Health EHR-derived de-identified database (Oncology Electronic Medical Records linked to mortality data via SSDMF)

Study Period: Jan 2016 – Dec 2023

Index Date: Date of 1L systemic therapy initiation in metastatic setting

Cohort: Adult patients (≥18) with confirmed mTNBC (ER−/PR−/HER2−, Stage IV or metastatic recurrence)

Primary Endpoints: Treatment patterns (regimen, sequencing, duration); TTNT per LOT; OS from 1L index date

Secondary: Dose modifications, treatment discontinuation reasons, real-world ORR (where available)

Cohort Eligibility — Inclusion & Exclusion

Inclusion Criteria

  • Female patients (male BC excluded — too few for analysis)
  • Age ≥18 at metastatic index date
  • Confirmed TNBC: ER−, PR−, HER2− (IHC/FISH confirmed, not missing)
  • Metastatic (Stage IV) breast cancer — de novo or recurrent
  • At least one systemic anti-cancer therapy after metastatic diagnosis
  • ≥2 clinical visits in Flatiron system (activity filter — ensures adequate data capture)
  • ≥6 months of follow-up available after index date (or death)

Exclusion Criteria

  • Prior malignancy within 5 years (other than non-melanoma skin cancer, cervical in situ)
  • Biomarker status not confirmed (ER/PR/HER2 missing or equivocal) — sensitivity analysis with these included
  • HER2-low patients (IHC 1+ or 2+/FISH−) — analyzed separately in sub-study
  • Enrolled in clinical trial at index date (may distort real-world patterns)
  • Missing index date or no drug administration records post-index

Cohort Build — Step by Step SQL Logic

Step 1 — Identify TNBC Patients with Metastatic Diagnosis SQL Pseudocode 
WITH tnbc_patients AS (

  SELECT
    p.patient_id,
    p.date_of_birth,
    p.sex,
    p.race,
    p.ethnicity,

    -- Metastatic index date: earliest Stage IV diagnosis
    MIN(cd.diagnosis_date) AS met_index_date,
    cd.de_novo_flag,  -- 1 = de novo Stage IV, 0 = recurrent

    -- Biomarker confirmation (confirmed NEGATIVE for all three)
    br_er.result_value  AS er_status,
    br_pr.result_value  AS pr_status,
    br_her2.result_value AS her2_status

  FROM flatiron.patient p

  JOIN flatiron.cancer_diagnosis cd
    ON p.patient_id = cd.patient_id
    AND cd.cancer_type   = 'Breast'
    AND cd.stage_group   = 'IV'

  -- Confirm ER negative (not missing, not equivocal)
  JOIN flatiron.biomarker_result br_er
    ON p.patient_id = br_er.patient_id
    AND br_er.biomarker_name = 'ER'
    AND br_er.result_value   = 'Negative'

  -- Confirm PR negative
  JOIN flatiron.biomarker_result br_pr
    ON p.patient_id = br_pr.patient_id
    AND br_pr.biomarker_name = 'PR'
    AND br_pr.result_value   = 'Negative'

  -- Confirm HER2 negative (IHC 0/1+ or IHC 2+ with negative FISH)
  JOIN flatiron.biomarker_result br_her2
    ON p.patient_id = br_her2.patient_id
    AND br_her2.biomarker_name  = 'HER2'
    AND br_her2.result_value    = 'Negative'

  WHERE
    p.sex = 'Female'
    AND DATEDIFF(year, p.date_of_birth, cd.diagnosis_date) >= 18

  GROUP BY
    p.patient_id, p.date_of_birth, p.sex, p.race, p.ethnicity,
    cd.de_novo_flag, br_er.result_value, br_pr.result_value, br_her2.result_value
)
Step 2 — Assign First-Line Therapy & Index Date SQL Pseudocode 
-- Get first systemic treatment on or up to 30 days BEFORE met diagnosis
-- (covers cases where treatment started while awaiting formal Stage IV staging)

first_line AS (
  SELECT
    da.patient_id,
    da.drug_name,
    da.regimen_name,   -- curated regimen label from Flatiron
    da.administration_date AS lot1_start_date,

    -- Classify regimen type
    CASE
      WHEN da.regimen_name LIKE '%pembrolizumab%'   THEN 'IO_Combo'
      WHEN da.regimen_name LIKE '%atezolizumab%'    THEN 'IO_Combo'
      WHEN da.drug_class   = 'Taxane'               THEN 'Taxane_Mono_Combo'
      WHEN da.drug_class   = 'Platinum'             THEN 'Platinum_Based'
      WHEN da.drug_class   = 'PARP_Inhibitor'       THEN 'PARP_Inhibitor'
      ELSE 'Other_Chemo'
    END AS lot1_regimen_class,

    ROW_NUMBER() OVER (
      PARTITION BY da.patient_id
      ORDER BY da.administration_date ASC
    ) AS rn

  FROM flatiron.drug_administration da
  JOIN tnbc_patients tp ON da.patient_id = tp.patient_id

  WHERE
    da.administration_date >= DATEADD(day, -30, tp.met_index_date)
    AND da.administration_date <= DATEADD(day, 60, tp.met_index_date)
    AND da.drug_category = 'Systemic Antineoplastic'
    AND da.treatment_setting = 'Metastatic'
    AND da.clinical_trial_flag = 0  -- exclude trial patients
)

SELECT * FROM first_line WHERE rn = 1;  -- keep first drug only

outcomes AS (
  SELECT
    f.patient_id,
    f.lot1_start_date,
    f.lot1_regimen_class,

    -- Next treatment start (LOT2)
    lot2.lot2_start_date,
    DATEDIFF(day, f.lot1_start_date, lot2.lot2_start_date) AS ttnt_days,

    -- Overall Survival
    m.death_date,
    COALESCE(m.death_date, m.last_known_alive_date) AS os_end_date,
    DATEDIFF(day, f.lot1_start_date,
      COALESCE(m.death_date, m.last_known_alive_date)
    ) AS os_days,

    -- Event flags (1 = event occurred, 0 = censored)
    CASE WHEN lot2.lot2_start_date IS NOT NULL THEN 1 ELSE 0 END AS ttnt_event,
    CASE WHEN m.death_date IS NOT NULL THEN 1 ELSE 0 END AS os_event,

    -- Pre/post sacituzumab approval (April 7, 2021)
    CASE
      WHEN f.lot1_start_date < '2021-04-07' THEN 'Pre_Approval'
      ELSE 'Post_Approval'
    END AS approval_era

  FROM first_line f

  LEFT JOIN lot2_starts lot2
    ON f.patient_id = lot2.patient_id

  LEFT JOIN flatiron.enhanced_mortality m
    ON f.patient_id = m.patient_id
)

Common Methodological Challenges & Solutions

Immortal Time Bias

Problem: If you define cohort by later event (e.g., reaching 2L) but count survival from an earlier time, patients must survive to reach 2L — artificially inflating survival in the 2L+ group.

Solution: Use time-conditional analyses. For 2L outcomes, set index date at 2L start, not 1L start. Or use landmark analyses.

Informative Censoring

Problem: Patients who leave the Flatiron network (e.g., transferred care, hospice) are censored but may not be random — sicker patients may leave earlier.

Solution: Use enhanced mortality (SSDMF) to recover deaths. Define administrative censoring date. Conduct sensitivity analyses with different censoring rules.

Confounding by Indication

Problem: Patients who receive sacituzumab govitecan post-approval may differ systematically from those who received other 2L agents pre-approval (younger, better PS, more lines prior).

Solution: Propensity score matching or inverse probability of treatment weighting (IPTW) using baseline covariates (age, ECOG, metastatic sites, prior lines).

Biomarker Misclassification

Problem: NLP-extracted ER/PR/HER2 status may misclassify HR+ patients as TNBC or vice versa. Multiple test results per patient may conflict.

Solution: Use most recent confirmed biomarker closest to metastatic diagnosis. Implement hierarchy (IHC then FISH). Run sensitivity analysis with broader/stricter definitions.

Disease & Clinical Knowledge

Q What defines triple-negative breast cancer and why is it the most challenging subtype to treat? ›
TNBC is defined by the absence of estrogen receptor (ER), progesterone receptor (PR), and HER2 expression — confirmed by IHC (ER/PR <1% staining) and IHC/FISH (HER2). It is the most challenging subtype because:
  • It lacks the three main therapeutic targets that drive treatment in other subtypes (endocrine therapy and HER2-directed agents are not effective)
  • It is biologically heterogeneous — "TNBC" is actually a collection of different molecular subtypes (BL1, BL2, IM, M, MSL, LAR)
  • It tends to be more aggressive, with rapid progression and higher rates of visceral metastases
  • Response to chemotherapy can be dramatic initially but relapses are common and fast
  • Until recently (~2019–2021), chemotherapy was the only option; now immunotherapy (pembrolizumab for PD-L1+ patients) and ADCs (sacituzumab govitecan) and PARP inhibitors (for gBRCA patients) have expanded the landscape
In the metastatic setting, median OS was approximately 12–18 months in the pre-IO era — the worst prognosis of any metastatic breast cancer subtype.
Q Explain TNM staging and how it informs your cohort definition in an mTNBC study. ›
TNM is the AJCC staging system: T = tumor size (T1 ≤20mm → T4 chest wall invasion), N = regional lymph node involvement (N0 = none → N3 = ≥10 nodes), M = distant metastasis (M0 = none, M1 = present). Stage IV = any T, any N, M1.

For an mTNBC cohort definition:
  • The metastatic index date is anchored to the first Stage IV (M1) diagnosis in the Flatiron system
  • I must distinguish de novo Stage IV (first presentation with metastatic disease) from recurrent metastatic disease (previously Stage I–III, now progressed to Stage IV) — because prior treatment history differs and affects how I count lines of therapy
  • I also use the AJCC 8th edition awareness — biomarker-modified staging means some Stage II/III anatomic patients are re-staged pathologically. This can create inconsistencies in Flatiron staging fields if clinical vs pathologic staging is mixed
  • I validate metastatic status with evidence of distant metastatic sites (bone, liver, lung, brain) from the metastatic diagnosis table in Flatiron as a supplementary check
Q What is the current standard of care for 1L mTNBC? How has it changed since 2019? ›
Pre-2019: Chemotherapy only — taxanes (paclitaxel, nab-paclitaxel), gemcitabine/carboplatin, anthracyclines if not previously used. No approved targeted agents except for gBRCA patients (olaparib approved 2018).

2019–2021 (IO era):
  • Atezolizumab + nab-paclitaxel — FDA approved March 2019 for PD-L1+ mTNBC (IMpassion130). Approval later withdrawn in US (2021) after IMpassion131 failed and updated ITT analysis showed no OS benefit. Still used in some countries.
  • Sacituzumab govitecan (Trodelvy) — FDA approved April 2021 for ≥2 prior lines (accelerated), full approval November 2021 (ASCENT). Game changer for 2L+ mTNBC.
  • Pembrolizumab + chemotherapy — FDA approved November 2021 for 1L unresectable/metastatic TNBC with CPS ≥10 (KEYNOTE-355). Current SOC for PD-L1+ patients.
Current SOC (2024): For PD-L1 CPS ≥10: pembrolizumab + taxane (nab-paclitaxel or paclitaxel) or carboplatin. For PD-L1 negative or unknown: taxane-based chemotherapy ± platinum. For gBRCA1/2: PARP inhibitors are an option in any line.

RWE Methods

Q How do you define line of therapy in a real-world mTNBC treatment patterns study? What challenges arise? ›
Line of therapy (LOT) assignment is one of the most critical methodological decisions in treatment patterns research. My approach:
  • Index date: First systemic antineoplastic therapy on/after metastatic diagnosis (allow 30-day look-back)
  • Line break criteria: A new LOT begins when (a) a new drug not in the current regimen is initiated after a treatment gap (≥30–90 days — must define and justify), or (b) clinical documentation of progression and new treatment
  • Maintenance continuations: If pembrolizumab continues as maintenance after chemo stops, count as same LOT, not new
  • Combination regimens: Multi-drug regimens started within a window (e.g., 28 days) count as one LOT
Key challenges:
  • No universal consensus on gap threshold — sensitivity analyses with 30/60/90-day gaps are important
  • Oral agents (capecitabine, olaparib) may be missed in IV-capture-only data — causes underestimation of LOTs
  • Treatment holiday vs discontinuation — a long gap may be a planned break or true EOT; can't always distinguish
  • Prior adjuvant/neoadjuvant therapy must be handled carefully — does a prior taxane in the curative setting "count" as Line 1? Generally no — LOT counting starts at metastatic diagnosis
Q What is immortal time bias and how would it appear in your mTNBC study? ›
Definition: Immortal time bias occurs when the follow-up period during which patients cannot experience the outcome (because they haven't yet received the exposure being studied) is incorrectly attributed to the exposed group.

mTNBC Example: Suppose I want to study OS among mTNBC patients who received sacituzumab govitecan vs. those who did not. If I define the "sacituzumab govitecan" group by any receipt of it (even if received at 2L or 3L) and start follow-up from metastatic diagnosis, then the time patients spend before receiving sacituzumab — during which they must have survived — is miscounted as "time on sacituzumab." This artificially makes the sacituzumab group look like it has better survival.

Fixes:
  • Set the index date at the time of sacituzumab govitecan initiation, not at metastatic diagnosis
  • Use a time-varying covariate approach where sacituzumab exposure is entered dynamically
  • Use landmark analysis: assess outcomes only from a fixed time point (e.g., 6 months after index) among survivors at that point
  • For treatment patterns studies (not comparative): report TTNT from each LOT's start date, not from the initial index
Q Why would you use TTNT instead of PFS as a primary endpoint in your Flatiron mTNBC study? ›
PFS limitations in RWE: PFS requires imaging-based evidence of disease progression per RECIST criteria — structured, protocol-mandated imaging with standardized measurement. In clinical practice, imaging frequency, timing, and reporting vary enormously. Radiologic progression is not typically recorded as a structured data field in EHR systems; it requires NLP extraction from radiology notes, which has known sensitivity/specificity limitations.

Why TTNT works:
  • TTNT (time from current LOT start → next LOT start) is fully captured from drug administration records — no NLP required
  • Reflects a clinician's real-world decision to change therapy, which is usually triggered by progression or intolerance
  • Practical and reproducible across different Flatiron studies — enables benchmarking
Limitations of TTNT I must acknowledge:
  • Cannot distinguish discontinuation due to progression vs. toxicity — both result in same "TTNT event." A patient who stops due to toxicity and switches isn't progressing but is counted the same way
  • If patient dies before receiving next treatment, event may be death (or censoring) not treatment switch — OS must be analyzed jointly
  • Patients who discontinue and go to hospice with no further treatment are censored — these may be the worst outcomes, causing informative censoring bias
In our study, I report TTNT as primary and also report treatment duration (TTD) and OS as secondary endpoints to triangulate effectiveness.
Q How do you handle biomarker missingness in your TNBC cohort definition using Flatiron? ›
This is a critical challenge because TNBC is defined by absence of markers — a confirmed negative is different from a missing result.

My approach:
  • Primary cohort: Require explicitly confirmed negative status for all three markers (ER−, PR−, HER2−) from at least one pathology report closest to metastatic diagnosis
  • HER2 hierarchy: If IHC 2+, require FISH/ISH negative to confirm HER2−. IHC 0 or 1+ is sufficient for HER2−. Document this rule clearly.
  • Most recent test: If multiple biomarker results exist, use the most recent one prior to or at metastatic diagnosis. Biomarker status can change with metastatic recurrence (discordance between primary and metastatic tumor)
  • Flatiron data caveat: Biomarker data in Flatiron comes from both structured fields and NLP-extracted pathology reports. Confirm which source is being used and its extraction accuracy
Sensitivity analyses:
  • Include patients with ≥2 of 3 confirmed negative markers (test sensitivity of definition)
  • Separately analyze those with equivocal HER2 (IHC 2+/FISH unknown) as "probable TNBC"
  • Report missingness rates by biomarker and by calendar year (data quality typically improves over time in Flatiron)
Q Walk me through how you would analyze pre- vs post-sacituzumab govitecan approval treatment patterns in mTNBC. ›
FDA approval date: April 7, 2021 (accelerated approval for mTNBC after ≥2 prior therapies).

Study era definition:
  • Pre-approval era: 1L start date before April 7, 2021
  • Post-approval era: 1L start date on or after April 7, 2021
  • Alternatively, define by 2L start date (since sacituzumab is 2L+ — patients who started 2L before vs. after approval)
What to compare:
  • Distribution of 2L regimens pre vs post approval — expect shift toward sacituzumab govitecan post-April 2021
  • Time from 1L end to 2L start — did adoption delay 2L initiation?
  • Patient characteristics by era — post-approval patients may be fitter (new drug access) or sicker (saved for new option)
  • OS and TTNT by era
Methodological considerations:
  • Calendar time confounding — secular trends in supportive care, other new drugs may co-occur. Adjust for year of treatment start
  • Selection bias — post-approval era patients may not have reached 2L in follow-up yet (administrative censoring). Require ≥6 months follow-up or use time-to-event methods
  • Ramp-up period — drug uptake grows over time post-approval. Consider a ≥6-month post-approval window as the "post era" to capture meaningful utilization
Q What are the key limitations of Flatiron Health data that you would cite in a study publication? ›
  • Non-random selection: Flatiron captures patients treated at oncology practices using Flatiron's EHR software — predominantly community oncology. Academic centers and integrated health systems may be underrepresented, limiting generalizability.
  • Incomplete oral drug capture: IV chemotherapy and infused biologics are well-captured via drug administration records. Oral agents (capecitabine, olaparib, talazoparib) rely on prescription records, which have lower and variable completeness.
  • Mortality ascertainment: Death is captured via Social Security Death Master File (SSDMF) linkage + EHR records. SSDMF is known to miss ~5–10% of US deaths. Use of enhanced mortality data improves but doesn't fully resolve this.
  • Missing biomarker data: Especially for newer biomarkers (PD-L1 CPS, BRCA, TMB) — missingness can be 30–60%. These may not be missing at random (sicker patients may not be tested).
  • No patient-reported outcomes: Quality of life, symptom burden, patient preference for treatment are not captured.
  • NLP limitations: Unstructured clinical notes are processed by NLP models with finite accuracy. Staging, response, and some biomarker data extracted from notes may contain errors.
  • Confounding by indication: Treatment is not randomized. Sicker patients may receive less aggressive therapy. Unmeasured confounders cannot be fully adjusted for — propensity methods address measured confounding only.
  • Healthcare access & insurance: Not captured. Patients in Flatiron are actively receiving care — under-represents patients with access barriers who may be untreated or lost to follow-up.
Q What is pCR and in what context would it appear in your mTNBC RWE work? ›
pCR (Pathologic Complete Response) is defined as no residual invasive cancer in the breast and axillary lymph nodes at the time of surgery following neoadjuvant (pre-surgical) chemotherapy. The standard definition is ypT0/is ypN0 (AJCC).

Clinical significance in TNBC: pCR after neoadjuvant chemo is a strong surrogate for improved event-free survival (EFS) and OS in early-stage TNBC. Patients who achieve pCR have significantly better long-term outcomes.

Where pCR appears in mTNBC RWE:
  • Patient history: Recurrent mTNBC patients in your cohort may have prior neoadjuvant chemo records. Whether they achieved pCR affects residual chemo sensitivity in the metastatic setting.
  • Treatment decision driver: Patients who did NOT achieve pCR (residual disease) after neoadjuvant anthracycline/taxane are now eligible for capecitabine (CREATE-X trial data). This prior treatment history affects 1L metastatic eligibility criteria.
  • Adjuvant therapy context: Post-neoadjuvant adjuvant pembrolizumab (KEYNOTE-522 regimen) is now SOC. These patients may enter metastatic studies having already received IO — important for prior IO exposure covariate.
  • NOT a primary RWE endpoint in mTNBC studies — it's an early-stage endpoint. But it's a key patient characteristic variable in recurrent mTNBC cohorts.

Use the STAR + Methods framework:

  • Objective: "Our primary objective was to describe treatment patterns across lines of therapy and estimate real-world TTNT and OS from 1L initiation."
  • Data & Design: "We used Flatiron Health's EHR-derived de-identified database — retrospective cohort study, 2019–2023, community oncology settings."
  • Cohort: "Patients were required to have confirmed TNBC (ER−/PR−/HER2−), Stage IV diagnosis or metastatic recurrence, and ≥1 systemic therapy post-index. We excluded clinical trial participants."
  • Key Methods: "We defined LOT using drug administration records with 90-day gap rule, used Kaplan-Meier for TTNT and OS, and compared pre/post approval eras. We addressed immortal time bias by indexing at each LOT start."
  • Results/Insight: "Post-approval, sacituzumab use at 2L increased from 2% to ~40% within 12 months. Real-world TTNT on sacituzumab was approximately X months vs. X months on other 2L agents — shorter than trial due to less selected population."
  • Impact: "Findings were used to inform market access strategy and supported label expansion discussions with payers."
